ABSTRACT
The SmartFarming Decision Support System is a machine learning based prototype that helps farmers and agriculture planners choose an appropriate crop and a supporting fertilizer plan by using observable field conditions. In many real scenarios, crop selection decisions are made using experience, local advice, or generalized seasonal expectations. While these approaches are valuable, they often do not explicitly account for measurable soil nutrients, weather patterns, and location context at the same time. This can result in crop–condition mismatch, inefficient fertilizer usage, reduced yield stability, and unnecessary financial risk. 

This project addresses the decision gap by combining a trained classification pipeline with a rule-based fertilizer recommendation layer and a simple web interface. The system accepts inputs such as Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium, temperature, humidity, soil pH, rainfall, soil moisture, organic matter, sunlight hours, altitude, soil type, agro‑climatic region, state, season, and irrigation method. A Random Forest classifier produces a crop recommendation and confidence ranking, while a profile-driven logic layer produces fertilizer guidance and attention points when values fall outside preferred ranges. The overall aim is not to automate farming, but to provide a transparent, fast, and repeatable decision support workflow that improves planning and encourages sustainable resource use.

1 INTRODUCTION
Agriculture remains one of the most important sectors for food security, rural livelihoods, and economic stability. However, many farm-level decisions—especially crop selection and nutrient planning—are often made under uncertainty. The same crop can perform very differently across regions and seasons due to changes in soil fertility, rainfall patterns, temperature ranges, humidity levels, and irrigation access. When the decision is based only on tradition or partial knowledge, the outcome can be crop failure, lower yield, higher input cost, and degraded soil health from incorrect fertilizer use.

The SmartFarming Decision Support System is designed as a practical bridge between measurable field conditions and actionable recommendations. The project provides a workflow in which a user enters soil nutrient indicators and environmental/context variables, and the system returns a recommended crop with a confidence score, alternative crop suggestions, and a fertilizer plan aligned with crop profiles. The central goal is decision support—not replacement of farmers’ expertise—by providing an additional evidence-based viewpoint that is quick to use and consistent across repeated evaluations. The system is implemented as a machine learning pipeline combined with a rule-based fertilizer logic layer and delivered through a simple Flask web interface and JSON API endpoints.


1.1 CHALLENGES IN TRADITIONAL FARMING DECISION-MAKING
Traditional crop planning frequently relies on prior experience, neighbor guidance, and high-level seasonal expectations. While these sources of knowledge are valuable, they can struggle to adapt to rapidly changing climate patterns, irregular rainfall distribution, or soil nutrient depletion over repeated cultivation cycles. In many locations, the decision process also suffers from limited access to up-to-date soil test results and the absence of structured tools that combine soil, weather, and location constraints into a single recommendation. As a result, farmers may choose a crop that is popular in the region but not optimal for their specific nutrient status or water availability.

Another challenge is fertilizer planning. Fertilizers are sometimes applied using “one-size-fits-all” rules or vendor suggestions, which can lead to over-application (increasing cost and runoff risk) or under-application (reducing yield potential). Additionally, the underlying decision logic is often not documented in a measurable way, making it hard to evaluate and improve over time. Finally, even when digital advisory apps exist, they may provide generic guidance without transparency, or they require complex inputs that reduce usability in real farm settings.

These challenges motivate a system that is input-aware, explainable, and consistent, so that crop and fertilizer suggestions become defensible decisions grounded in measurable conditions.


1.2 AN AI-BASED SOLUTION FOR SMART FARMING
Artificial Intelligence and machine learning techniques are well suited for decision support problems where multiple interacting variables influence the outcome. Crop suitability is a classic example: nutrient balance, soil characteristics, temperature, humidity, rainfall, and farm management practices interact in nonlinear ways. A machine learning model can learn patterns across many examples and produce a recommendation even when the relationships are not easily captured by simple thresholds.

In this project, a Random Forest classifier is used as the core predictive model because it handles nonlinear relationships and mixed-feature inputs effectively after appropriate preprocessing. The system first validates user inputs and transforms them through a pipeline that includes numeric and categorical imputation, one-hot encoding for categorical features, and mutual-information based feature selection. This reduces noise and keeps the model focused on the most informative variables. The trained model outputs a predicted crop class and also provides probability scores for each crop class, allowing the system to show a ranked list of top alternatives rather than a single opaque answer.

The AI layer is intentionally combined with a rule-based fertilizer guidance layer. This hybrid approach provides both predictive power (crop recommendation) and deterministic clarity (fertilizer suggestions and caution notes), improving trust and interpretability for end users.


1.3 AUTOMATED RECOMMENDATIONS AND RESPONSIBLE DATA USE
A decision support system should not only generate predictions; it should communicate recommendations in a way that supports responsible action. For smart farming, this means translating model outputs into clear recommendations while highlighting uncertainty and constraints. The SmartFarming Decision Support System provides a predicted crop along with a confidence percentage and the top alternative crops. This reduces over-reliance on a single label and allows the user to consider practical trade-offs such as market demand, seed availability, and labor constraints.

The system also generates “matched conditions” and “attention points.” Matched conditions indicate which soil and climate inputs align with the recommended crop profile, while attention points highlight values outside preferred ranges (for example, rainfall or nutrient levels being above or below typical bands). These attention points function as lightweight alerts, encouraging the user to review inputs, consider mitigation steps, or treat the recommendation as provisional.

Responsible data use is also important. The system is designed so that it can run using manual inputs without collecting personal data. When deployed, it should store only what is necessary, avoid unnecessary location tracking, and communicate that recommendations are advisory. Ethical monitoring in this context means transparency: showing what inputs were used, what was predicted, and what limitations exist (for example, dependence on the training dataset’s coverage).


2 LITERATURE REVIEW
Smart farming and precision agriculture have become active research and development areas due to the increasing availability of agricultural data and the need to improve productivity sustainably. Literature in this domain covers sensor-based monitoring, weather-driven advisory systems, machine learning for crop and yield prediction, and recommender systems for fertilizer and irrigation planning. Many studies highlight that crop suitability is influenced not only by macronutrients (N, P, K) but also by climatic and management conditions such as rainfall patterns, temperature ranges, humidity, soil pH, and irrigation strategies.

Machine learning approaches commonly discussed include decision trees, Random Forest, support vector machines, gradient boosting, and neural networks. A recurring conclusion is that tree-based ensemble methods perform competitively on tabular agronomic datasets, especially when interpretability and robustness matter. Researchers also emphasize the importance of preprocessing, feature encoding, and validation methods such as cross-validation to avoid overfitting and to ensure reliable generalization.

In parallel, literature on decision support systems stresses usability and trust. Farmers and extension workers prefer tools that provide simple interfaces, explainable reasoning, and actionable outputs instead of raw predictions. This project’s design choices—hybrid ML + rule-based logic, a web interface, and a compact feature set—are aligned with these findings and aim to provide a balanced, presentation-ready prototype.


2.1 REVIEW OF LITERATURE
Existing work in crop recommendation often frames the problem as multi-class classification, where the target is the most suitable crop under given soil and climatic conditions. Early systems relied on expert rules and static threshold tables, but these can be difficult to maintain and may not capture complex interactions among variables. As datasets became available, researchers increasingly adopted supervised machine learning, demonstrating that models can learn nuanced suitability patterns from historical or simulated data.

Tree-based ensembles such as Random Forest are frequently reported as strong baselines for agronomic tabular datasets. Reasons include their ability to handle nonlinear relationships, resilience to noise, and support for feature importance measures that aid explanation. Studies also discuss the role of categorical context features—such as region, season, and irrigation method—because the same nutrient levels can lead to different recommendations depending on climate zone and cropping calendar.

For fertilizer recommendation, literature typically separates two approaches: (1) predictive models that output fertilizer quantities directly and (2) rule-based engines that map soil test deficiencies to corrective inputs. Many practical DSS implementations choose rule-based fertilizer guidance because it is easier to explain and aligns with agronomic extension practices. 

Another major theme is evaluation. Accuracy alone is often insufficient; researchers report precision, recall, F1-score, confusion matrices, and cross-validation results to understand class-wise behavior and stability. Finally, deployment studies stress that a lightweight web or mobile interface improves adoption by reducing technical barriers. These strands of literature collectively support the design of the SmartFarming Decision Support System.


2.2 CONCLUSIONS FROM THE LITERATURE REVIEW
From the reviewed literature, three conclusions are most relevant to this project. First, crop suitability prediction is well modeled as a multi-variable decision problem, and ensemble classifiers are a strong fit when the dataset is structured and tabular. Second, usability and interpretability strongly influence whether a system becomes practically useful; therefore, outputs should include explanations, alternatives, and attention notes rather than a single label. Third, reliable evaluation requires more than a single train/test score: cross-validation, class-wise reports, and confusion matrices provide stronger evidence of generalization and help identify overlapping crop profiles.

The literature also indicates common gaps in many academic prototypes. Some systems rely on limited features (only NPK and pH), while others assume continuous sensor integration without providing fallback for manual inputs. Several systems produce recommendations but do not show confidence or error patterns, which reduces trust during adoption. 

The SmartFarming Decision Support System addresses these gaps in a focused way. It combines nutrient, weather, location, and management inputs; reports a ranked prediction list with confidence; and provides a deterministic fertilizer guidance layer for explainability. Additionally, it follows a reproducible pipeline approach (preprocessing → selection → model) and stores evaluation artifacts (metrics summary, feature importance, confusion matrix). These design choices align with literature recommendations for building decision tools that are both accurate and understandable.


3 SYSTEM ANALYSIS
System analysis defines what the SmartFarming Decision Support System must achieve, who uses it, and what constraints shape the implementation. The primary users are farmers, agricultural students, and extension workers who need rapid guidance on crop choice and fertilizer planning for a given set of conditions. The core functional requirement is to accept field inputs and return a recommended crop, alternative crops with ranking, and a fertilizer plan that considers nutrient balance and crop profile ranges. Non-functional requirements include usability, responsiveness, transparency of outputs, and reliability of input validation.

The analysis also considers constraints. The current system is a prototype and does not integrate directly with sensors or live weather APIs; instead, it relies on user-provided values and a reproducible dataset pipeline. This constraint is acceptable for a project-level DSS because it allows controlled evaluation and clear demonstration of the machine learning workflow. Another constraint is the scope of supported crops and regions. The model supports a fixed set of crop classes and uses categorical fields like region, state, season, and irrigation method for contextualization. 

Finally, system analysis highlights risks: incorrect user inputs, shifting climate behavior, and differences between generated/training data and real-world farms. The system mitigates these through input bounds checking, confidence ranking, and caution notes.


3.1 EXISTING SYSTEM
The existing approach to crop and fertilizer decision-making in many contexts is largely manual and experience-based. Farmers often select crops using historical patterns, local market trends, and advice from neighbors or vendors. While these heuristics can work, they typically do not account for measured soil nutrient status or for changing climate patterns that can alter crop performance from year to year. Even when soil testing is available, results might be interpreted informally or not integrated into a structured recommendation process.

Digital advisory systems exist, but many are either too generic or too complex. Generic systems provide broad seasonal crop lists without considering farm-specific nutrient balance, soil moisture, or micro-climate conditions. Complex systems may require continuous sensor inputs or paid subscriptions, which can reduce accessibility. Another limitation is that fertilizer suggestions are often not aligned with crop-specific nutrient ranges; farmers may apply fertilizers based on habit rather than deficiency-based correction, increasing the risk of nutrient runoff or soil imbalance.

The existing system also typically lacks transparent performance evidence. Farmers and students may not see any quantitative validation (accuracy, cross-validation, confusion matrices) that supports why a recommendation is reliable. These limitations motivate a new solution that is measurable, explainable, and easy to use.


3.2 PROPOSED SYSTEM
The proposed SmartFarming Decision Support System combines machine learning prediction with rule-based agronomic guidance and delivers the result through a web interface. The system accepts a complete set of inputs covering soil nutrients (N, P, K, pH, moisture, organic matter), weather profile (temperature, humidity, rainfall, sunlight, altitude), and location/management context (soil type, region, state, season, irrigation method). Inputs are validated using bounds and option lists to prevent unrealistic values.

After validation, inputs are passed into a trained pipeline that performs preprocessing and classification. Numeric fields are imputed using median strategy when needed, categorical fields use most-frequent imputation and one-hot encoding, and mutual-information based feature selection retains the most informative transformed features. A Random Forest classifier predicts the crop label and also produces class probabilities, enabling ranking of the top alternatives. 

The output layer includes a deterministic fertilizer recommendation engine that uses crop profiles and compares the user’s inputs against preferred ranges. When values are low for a nutrient, the engine proposes corrective fertilizer components (e.g., Urea for nitrogen) and generates attention notes. The final user output includes the recommended crop, confidence, top alternatives, fertilizer plan, matched conditions, attention points, and an input summary. The system is accessible through both a browser UI and a JSON API endpoint.

4 SYSTEM DESIGN
System design translates the analysis into an implementable architecture with clear modules, data flow, and responsibilities. The SmartFarming Decision Support System follows a pipeline-oriented design: user inputs are collected, validated, transformed into a model-ready representation, and then passed through a trained classifier. The prediction is combined with a fertilizer rule engine to generate a complete advisory output. The architecture is designed to be modular so that individual parts (dataset generation, training, inference, UI rendering) can be improved independently without rewriting the entire system.

A key design choice is the separation of concerns. The machine learning pipeline is responsible only for predicting crop suitability from structured inputs. The fertilizer recommendation layer is a deterministic component that compares the same inputs to crop profile ranges and produces a practical nutrient correction plan and caution notes. The web layer focuses on user interaction: it renders forms, displays results, and provides JSON endpoints for programmatic use. 

The design also includes reproducibility features. The model artifact is saved as a serialized pipeline and can be loaded for inference without retraining. Evaluation artifacts (metrics JSON, classification report, plots) are stored in a report directory, allowing results to be presented and verified. This structure makes the project suitable for academic submission and future extension.


4.1 FARM DATA ACQUISITION
Data acquisition in this project includes two complementary pathways: (1) creating/training with a structured dataset and (2) collecting input values at prediction time. For training and evaluation, the system uses a dataset of crop suitability examples that contains numeric soil and climate variables and categorical context variables. In the current prototype, the dataset is generated programmatically using crop profiles that specify realistic ranges for nutrients, weather conditions, and management factors. Each crop class has a profile defining preferred bounds (for example, temperature or rainfall bands) and valid categorical contexts (regions, seasons, irrigation methods). From these profiles, multiple records per crop are sampled to build a balanced dataset.

For user interaction, data is acquired through a web form that groups inputs into soil nutrients, weather profile, and location/management. This design reduces input error by providing units, min/max bounds for numeric fields, and controlled options for categorical selections. The same set of feature columns is used both in training and in inference, ensuring alignment between model expectations and UI input.

Although live sensor integration is not part of the current implementation, the design supports it as future scope: the same features could be populated automatically using soil tests, IoT sensors, or weather APIs. The current focus remains on a robust and reproducible decision support prototype.


4.2 PREPROCESSING OF FARMING DATA
Preprocessing is essential because agricultural datasets often contain mixed feature types and may include missing values or inconsistent formatting. The SmartFarming Decision Support System uses a structured preprocessing pipeline that treats numeric and categorical features differently while producing a single model-ready feature matrix. Numeric inputs (such as N, P, K, temperature, humidity, rainfall, soil pH, soil moisture, organic matter, sunlight hours, and altitude) are processed using a median imputation strategy. This choice is robust to outliers and preserves central tendency without requiring manual filling.

Categorical inputs (soil type, agro-climatic region, state, season, irrigation method) are processed using most-frequent imputation followed by one-hot encoding. One-hot encoding converts categorical selections into binary indicator features while remaining robust to unseen categories through “handle unknown” behavior. After encoding, the feature space can grow; to keep the model compact and reduce redundancy, the pipeline applies mutual-information based feature selection (SelectPercentile). This step retains the most informative subset of transformed features, improving efficiency and potentially reducing overfitting.

The preprocessing design is implemented as part of a single scikit-learn Pipeline with a ColumnTransformer, ensuring that the same transformations are applied consistently during training and inference. This end-to-end pipeline is saved as a single artifact so deployment does not require re-implementing preprocessing logic separately.


4.3 CROP SUITABILITY PREDICTION AND RECOMMENDATION ENGINE
The crop prediction engine is the machine learning core of the system. It is implemented as a Random Forest classifier embedded in a scikit-learn Pipeline that includes preprocessing and feature selection. Random Forest is suitable here because crop suitability depends on nonlinear interactions among soil nutrients, climate variables, and categorical context. The ensemble nature of Random Forest also improves robustness, making it less sensitive to small variations in inputs.

During training, the dataset is split using a stratified 80/20 train–test split so that all crop classes remain represented. The system also applies 5‑fold stratified cross-validation on the training portion to estimate stability and reduce the risk of overly optimistic evaluation. After fitting the pipeline, the model predicts on the test split and computes standard classification metrics.

At inference time, the engine receives a single input record from the UI/API, converts it into a DataFrame, and calls predict() for the crop label. Additionally, the system calls predict_proba() to obtain probability scores over all crop classes. These probabilities are sorted to produce a ranked list of top alternatives (typically the top three) along with confidence percentages. This ranking is important because multiple crops can be suitable under similar conditions; presenting alternatives supports better decision-making and provides transparency about near-ties.

The prediction output is then passed to the fertilizer recommendation layer so that the final recommendation is not only “what crop” but also “how to support it” in terms of nutrients.


4.4 FERTILIZER SUGGESTION AND ALERT MECHANISM
Fertilizer suggestion in the SmartFarming Decision Support System is implemented using a rule-based approach driven by crop profiles. Each crop has preferred ranges for key nutrients and environmental variables, along with a base fertilizer plan. After the model predicts a crop, the rule engine compares the user’s input values against the preferred ranges for that crop. When a value falls within range, the system records it as a matched condition. When a value falls outside the range, the system creates an attention note describing whether it is above or below the preferred band.

For nutrient-related deficits, the system suggests corrective fertilizer components aligned with common agronomic practice. For example, low nitrogen can trigger addition of Urea, low phosphorus can trigger DAP, and low potassium can trigger MOP, while still preserving the base fertilizer plan for the predicted crop. The final fertilizer recommendation is presented as a combined plan, and duplicate suggestions are avoided through tokenization and de-duplication logic.

The “alert” concept here is deliberately lightweight: instead of sending external notifications, the system surfaces attention points directly on the result page. This makes the recommendation safer by encouraging review and preventing blind acceptance. It also provides an ethical safeguard: the system makes clear that not all inputs perfectly match and that corrective action or expert consultation may be needed for out-of-band conditions.


4.5 WEB INTERFACE AND API FOR VERIFICATION
The system is delivered through a Flask web application that supports both human-friendly interaction and programmatic access. The web interface includes a home page with grouped input sections (soil nutrients, weather profile, location and management) and a result page that displays the predicted crop, confidence score, fertilizer plan, alternative crop rankings, matched conditions, attention points, and an input summary. This design makes it easy to verify what was entered and how it influenced the recommendation.

In addition to HTML views, the system exposes JSON endpoints. A prediction API endpoint accepts a JSON payload and returns the same recommendation structure as the UI, enabling integration with other tools (for example, a mobile app or an agricultural dashboard). A health endpoint reports whether the trained model artifact and metrics file are available, which is useful for deployment checks and automated monitoring.

Verification is supported in multiple ways. First, input validation prevents unrealistic values by enforcing min/max bounds and fixed option lists. Second, the result view provides confidence and alternatives, which helps users judge uncertainty. Third, matched and caution conditions provide simple explainability. Together, these features improve trust and make the system suitable for demonstrations, lab evaluations, and project submission requirements.


4.6 MODULAR ARCHITECTURE
A modular architecture improves maintainability and supports future extensions. The SmartFarming Decision Support System is organized into distinct layers: configuration and paths management, schema and field definitions, dataset generation/loading, model training/evaluation, recommendation logic, crop profiles, and the Flask application layer. Each module has a single responsibility, which reduces coupling and makes it easier to test or enhance components independently.

For example, the schema module defines the feature columns, field metadata (labels, units, bounds), and grouping for the UI. The dataset builder is responsible for generating or loading the dataset in a consistent column order. The modeling module defines the preprocessing and Random Forest pipeline and produces evaluation artifacts (metrics JSON, plots, classification report). The recommendation module handles input coercion, model loading, probability ranking, and fertilizer suggestion via crop profiles. The Flask app module provides routing, template rendering, and API endpoints.

This separation enables clear upgrade paths. If new crops or regions are added, updates are primarily made to crop profiles and dataset generation logic. If a different model is evaluated, changes are localized to the modeling pipeline. If the UI is redesigned, templates and static assets can change without impacting model correctness. This modular approach is a good software engineering practice and supports the long-term evolution of the system.


4.7 PROPOSED EVALUATION METRICS
Evaluation metrics define how the system’s predictive performance and practical utility are measured. Since the crop recommendation problem is a multi-class classification task, the primary metric is overall accuracy on a held-out test split. However, accuracy alone may hide class-wise weaknesses, especially when certain crops have overlapping environmental profiles. Therefore, the system also reports weighted precision, weighted recall, and weighted F1-score, which account for both correctness and robustness across classes. 

To assess stability, the system performs k-fold cross-validation (stratified 5-fold) on the training data and reports the mean and standard deviation of accuracy. A low standard deviation suggests that performance is consistent across different splits, improving confidence that the model is not overly sensitive to how data is partitioned.

For deeper analysis, the system generates a confusion matrix. The confusion matrix provides class-wise insight by showing which crops are commonly confused with each other, which is essential in agronomic contexts where similar crop profiles exist. Additionally, the model produces a feature importance table based on Random Forest importance values after feature selection. This supports interpretability by indicating which variables (such as nitrogen, rainfall, sunlight hours, or season indicators) most influence predictions.

Finally, practical DSS utility is evaluated qualitatively through output clarity: the presence of confidence ranking, alternative crops, matched conditions, and attention points. These outputs act as “decision quality metrics” by improving transparency and supporting responsible action.


5 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
The results demonstrate that the SmartFarming Decision Support System achieves strong predictive performance while maintaining a user-friendly and explainable output format. On the prepared dataset of 1,920 records across 16 crop classes, the model achieves high accuracy on the held-out test split and shows stable behavior under cross-validation. This indicates that the combination of preprocessing, feature selection, and Random Forest classification captures meaningful patterns linking soil nutrients, climate variables, and contextual features to crop suitability.

Feature importance analysis suggests that both nutrient variables (such as nitrogen and potassium) and environmental variables (such as rainfall, sunlight hours, humidity, and temperature) significantly influence predictions. This aligns with agronomic expectations: crop suitability is rarely determined by a single factor, and the model appropriately uses multiple indicators. 

The confusion matrix shows that most crops are well separated, with remaining errors concentrated in classes that naturally overlap in environmental requirements. Such errors are expected and highlight the importance of presenting top alternatives and confidence rather than a single rigid label. The system’s hybrid design also improves practical value: even when the crop prediction is strong, the fertilizer recommendation layer adds actionable guidance and flags attention points when inputs are outside preferred ranges. Overall, the results support the system’s objective of being a reliable, presentation-ready decision support prototype.


5.1 RESULT
Quantitatively, the trained model produces the following evaluation outcomes on the current dataset: accuracy of 0.9609 (96.09%) on the test split, weighted precision of 0.9642, weighted recall of 0.9609, and weighted F1-score of 0.9612. The 5-fold cross-validation accuracy mean is 0.9525 with a standard deviation of 0.0098, suggesting consistent performance across splits. These metrics collectively indicate that the model generalizes well within the dataset’s scope.

The model also selects a compact subset of encoded features (44 selected features after percentile-based mutual-information selection). The top contributors include numeric nitrogen level, rainfall, sunlight hours, potassium level, soil moisture, humidity, temperature, altitude, phosphorus, and season indicators. This mix reinforces that both soil fertility and climatic context matter.

In terms of system output, the recommendation engine returns: (1) the predicted crop, (2) a confidence percentage, (3) top alternative crops, (4) a fertilizer plan derived from crop profiles and nutrient checks, and (5) matched and caution conditions. This output structure supports decision-making because it provides a rationale: it shows which inputs match the recommended crop profile and where corrective action may be needed.

Discussion of limitations is also important. The dataset used for training is generated from crop profiles and therefore reflects the assumptions in those profiles. Real-world validation on farm-collected data would be required for deployment. Nevertheless, the system effectively demonstrates the complete DSS pipeline and provides a strong baseline for future extension.

6 CONCLUSION
The SmartFarming Decision Support System demonstrates how machine learning and rule-based agronomic logic can be combined into a practical, explainable decision support tool for crop selection and fertilizer planning. The system accepts a comprehensive set of field inputs spanning soil nutrients, weather conditions, and contextual management factors, validates them, and produces a crop recommendation with confidence ranking. It further adds a fertilizer plan and attention points by comparing inputs to crop profile ranges. This hybrid output improves interpretability and makes the system more useful than a “black box” classifier alone.

Evaluation results on the prepared dataset show strong performance (around 96% test accuracy) and stable cross-validation behavior.
